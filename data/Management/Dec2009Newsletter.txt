<blockquote><meta http-equiv="Content-Type" content="text/html; charset=utf-8" /><title>OSG Newsletter</title><h1 align="left">OSG Newsletter, December 2009</h1><p align="left"><b>____________________________________________________________________________________________________________________________</b></p>   <br /><p><b>Special LHC Update from the Executive Director</b></p> <p><span class="caps">ATLAS</span> and <span class="caps">CMS</span> took event data and <span class="caps">OSG</span> performed excellently (again!) as the data was distributed across the world and analyzed.</p> <p>This is a result of an incredible amount of preparation and hard work, including many of you&mdash; from the experiments themselves, to the other sites and contributors, to the <span class="caps">OSG</span> and the <span class="caps">OSG</span> staff.</p> <p>The US Tier-1s andTier-2s&mdash;and some of the Tier-3s also&mdash;did their share and more!! While there will be no beam over the next couple of months, the experiments are working hard to get physics results&mdash;reprocessing the data several times, tweaking the Monte Carlo simulations to better understand the detector responses, etc. We must continue to be vigilant in ensuring our US portion of the distributed facility is second to none.</p> <p>The strength of the <span class="caps">OSG</span> lies in our collaborative approach and team spirit&mdash;bringing forward our deep and broad technical experience and contributions for the common good of research and many types of science, including the <span class="caps">LHC</span>.</p> <p>Below is part of the letter that project director Ian Bird sent for the end of the year:</p> <p>&ldquo;&hellip;We have finally seen real <a href="http://indico.cern.ch/conferenceDisplay.py?confId=76398">LHC data</a> move through the entire grid system in the way that we have been planning for the past several years; (In particular see the <span class="caps">ATLAS</span> and LHCb talks). The fact that the computing system is in such good shape at this stage is down to the fantastic efforts that have come from all of the Tier 1 and Tier 2 staff in putting in place all of the pieces of this rather complex system. This is an appropriate time to thank all of you for the huge efforts that you have made in the preparation and operation of this system: from the tireless discussions on middleware, the construction and operation of the many Tier 1 and Tier 2 sites, the building of links to many other projects, through to the very successful challenges of this year with both simulated and real data. These have all contributed to something that is unique &ndash; an operational global computing system for many thousands of physicists.</p> <p>Of course, this is just the starting point. The real challenges of <span class="caps">LHC</span> data are still to come. However, with the collaboration that we have built I am sure that we can look forward and respond to the challenges and excitement of the future.&rdquo;</p> <p>~ Ruth Pordes</p> <p>____________________________________________________________________________________________________________________________</p> <p><b>EINSTEIN at Home</b></p> <p>Caltech ranks high in Einstein at Home participation.</p> <p>The Gratia reports for the week of December 1st indicate five <a href="http://www.einsteinathome.org/">Einstein at Home</a> users utilizing thirty-one sites. 35221 jobs were completed with a 84.7% success rate. Wall clock hour consumption lies at 289669.3 with a 90% success rate. Einstein at Home reports 702,805.33843 recent average credits, a number that ranks the Caltech Open Science Grid team as third in the list of top participants for that week. The overall rank, based on accumulated credits, moved Caltech to the 11th spot, up one position from the previous week. Work has now begun to expand to Fermilab sites.</p> <p>~ Britta Daudert</p> <p>____________________________________________________________________________________________________________________________</p> <p><b>Adventures at IceCube</b></p> <table border="0" width="997">   <tbody>     <tr>       <td align="center" valign="top" width="251"><img src="/images/dom.jpg" alt="Digital Optical Model" height="187" width="250" /></td>       <td width="730">       <p>Steve Barnet, an <span class="caps">HPC</span> Administrator at the University of Wisconsin, was kind enough to share a bit of his experience traveling to the IceCube camp at the South Pole in November. The IceCube collaboration, which is building a telescope at the Pole to search for neutrinos, will use the <span class="caps">OSG</span> to perform computer simulations and analysis. Read the Adventures at IceCube <a href="http://www.opensciencegrid.org/icecube/">blog</a>. Steve welcomes comments and questions.</p>              <p>A Digital Optical Module (DOM). The IceCube detector will be made of 80 strings, each string containing 60 DOMS, drilled deep into the polar ice. (Photos courtesy NSF)</p>                     </td>     </tr>   </tbody> </table> <p>____________________________________________________________________________________________________________________________</p> <p><b>Benefits of grid software feed into reservoir modeling</b></p> <p>As oil and gas become increasingly difficult to find, petroleum engineers are forced to find more creative ways to seek out supplies. Consequently, the techniques used in the petroleum industry have become increasingly sophisticated. </p> <p>&ldquo;The technology is so complex that you really need a novel approach,&rdquo; said Ravi Vadapalli, a research scientist based at Texas Tech University&rsquo;s High Performance Computing Center. Vadapalli and Lloyd Heinze co-founded PEGrid (Petroleum Engineering Grid) in collaboration with three Texas universities, four software companies, and four oil companies; their goal is to train students in state-of-the-art petroleum engineering methods and integrated workflows to establish effective links between academic institutions and industry. </p> <p>PEGrid came into existence using techniques and tools created by TIGRE (Texas Internet Grid for Research and Education), a state-funded project based on the use of federally funded middleware &ndash; including Open Science Grid&rsquo;s Virtual Data Toolkit. </p> <p>Now PEGrid will use $45 million in software donations and private three-year grants to fill the gap between university researchers and industry application of grid technology. The bulk of this work will go towards developing new tools that can help academic and industry members better understand multiscale reservoir dynamics and train students in methods to predict the location and capacity of reservoirs based on existing data. </p> <p>~ Miriam Boon</p> <p>____________________________________________________________________________________________________________________________</p> <p><b>Production Report of CMS</b></p> <p>This has been a very exciting month for CMS&mdash;the Large Hadron Collider has not only circulated beam, but given us collisions at injection energies to analyze as well! Our overall consumption of CPU hours has decreased from roughly 1.7 million hours per week to around .7 million, attributed to the shift in focus from challenge data to real data. The LHC has now been shut down for maintenance; we expect to have collisions back in mid-Februrary. We&rsquo;re taking advantage of the interruption to deploy the latest CMS software release on the Scientific Linux 5 platform.</p> <p>~ Burt Holzman</p> <p>____________________________________________________________________________________________________________________________</p> <p><b>OSG Security News: A Season of Sharing, sharing the security news that is :)</b></p> <p> Happy Holidays everyone.</p> <p>The OSG security team has started a new <a href="http://osgsec.blogspot.com/">blog</a>, where we keep a track of security items daily. The blog is conveniently linked from both <a href="https://myosg.grid.iu.edu/abou">My OSG</a> under the Security News (upper right hand corner of the page) and from the opensciencegrid.org <a href="http://www.opensciencegrid.org/">home page</a> news box. The blog, open for public view, has summaries of current and recent security issues along with recommendations and tips. We recommend anyone interested in security to become a &ldquo;follower&rdquo; by clicking on the &ldquo;Follow&rdquo; link. All you need is a gmail, AIM, or yahoo account, and you can post comments to the blog.</p> <p>For restricted security material, we have included links to our security ticketing system. A security ticket contains details of sensitive security information, actions to take, tips, and anything else related to the issue. Any security contact registered with OSG can view the ticket contents, post questions, and answer others&rsquo; questions. You only need a DOEGrids certificate embedded in your browser and authorization will happen transparently.</p> <p>We strongly encourage all security contacts to use the blog to communicate with us and with their peers. Most people have similar questions and sometimes their peers s are much better at helping them than we are.</p> <p>~ Mine Altunay</p> <p>____________________________________________________________________________________________________________________________</p> <p><b>From the Executive Director</b></p> <p>As 2009 draws to a close, OSG is busier than ever. While the CPU usage has been steady over the past couple of months, the use by Engagement, LIGO, and other non-physics communities has increased and we inch towards a better understanding of how to provide shareable storage and local data management at the sites.</p> <p>We are building on the technical expertise, knowledge, and lessons learned to prepare for the next steps in our heterogeneous infrastructure, when the LHC experiments are likely to use a higher percentage of their own resources, when more new partners are building their local campus infrastructures to interface rather than integrate, and when new technologies in virtualization and scientific and commercial cloud resources are gaining in capability. An increasing number of people in the OSG teams are engaging in dialogs about the future of cyber-infrastructure at home and abroad. By the end of the year we will have talked to nearly all of the seven new members of the Science Advisory Group to get their input and feedback to on our plans and roadmaps.</p> <p>Once the DOE annual report is finalized in January, we will use sections in the newsletter to give you more depth about some of the accomplishments of the past year.</p> <p>In the meantime I wish you a restful, healthy, and peaceful holiday period.</p> <p> ~ Ruth Pordes</p> <p>____________________________________________________________________________________________________________________________</p>  <b>OSG
