<table bgcolor="#ffffcc" border="0" cellpadding="0" cellspacing="10" width="100%"><tbody><tr bordercolor="#999999" bgcolor="#ffffcc"><td valign="middle" width="146"><p><a href="http://www.opensciencegrid.org/"><img src="/images/osg-logo.gif" alt="OSG Logo" longdesc="/ADMIN_-_DO_NOT_EDIT/Edit_Page/https/www.opensciencegrid.org/images/osg_logo.gif" align="middle" border="0" height="82" width="144" /></a></p>               <p>&nbsp;</p>             </td>             <td align="left" valign="top" width="390">               <h1> </h1>               <h1>OSG Newsletter 2010: November &amp; December </h1>             </td>           </tr>         </tbody>       </table>       <table bgcolor="#fdebcf" border="0" cellpadding="0" cellspacing="10" width="100%">         <tbody>           <tr>             <td height="1748" valign="top" width="50%">               <p><b>Structural Biology Grid&#39;s Wide Search Molecular</b>               </p>               <p>Knowing the atomic resolution three dimensional                 structure of proteins is critical for understanding                 protein interactions. Discovering these structures is                 typically done using X-ray crystallography and a                 technique known as molecular replacement, which allows                 the protein crystal scattering pattern to be transformed                 into a 3D protein geometry by finding fragments of known                 proteins that effectively produce an equivalent                 scattering pattern. Unfortunately finding fragments that                 match can sometimes be very difficult, and in this case                 it is necessary to use alternative techniques that will                 typically involve months of additional work. </p>               <p>Enter Wide Search Molecular Replacement, a workflow                 developed in the lab of Piotr Sliz at Harvard Medical                 School. The group, part of the SBGrid VO, recently                 published work on using OSG to help solve the structure                 of proteins.</p>               <p>Via a publicly accessible <a href="https://portal.nebiogrid.org/secure/apps/wsmr/">web                   portal</a>, WS-MR allows users to specify a large                 model search set and parameter search space that can be                 used to perform years of computing in a few hours and to                 aggregate the data into a format that allows the user to                 quickly identify promising results. Although it provides                 a viable solution only about a quarter of the time, when                 it does, it typically saves the researcher weeks or                 months of wet lab or computational work.</p>               <p>Benefiting from opportunistic compute time available on                 OSG, WS-MR can rapidly initiate thousands of concurrent                 molecular replacement jobs. The group developed a                 particular model for the data flow 2 that minimizes data                 movement, ideally placing input data at each site once                 and streamlining output to a standard format that                 reduces the output data returned with each job and                 allows that data to be continuously integrated into live                 reports of the scientific results. This is important                 because a particular set of jobs in a protein study                 could run for several days. A key part of this system is                 the use of pilot jobs and glideinWMS, which have                 improved both reliability and scaling.</p>               <p>Since announcing the availability of the service                 several weeks ago there has been a steady demand from                 users and a backlog of jobs.</p>               <p>The group is now focusing on three areas: improving the                 ability to target searches, including the development of                 categorized &ldquo;search sets&rdquo;; creating a more full-featured                 web portal that will allow users to log in and better                 manage data, track jobs and securely share results; and                 exploring other computationally intensive aspects of                 structural biology, such as those for electron                 microscopy protein image processing, that can benefit                 from grid computing resources.</p>               <p>1, Sliz, Protein structure determination by exhaustive                 search of Protein Data Bank derived databases, Proc.                 Nat&rsquo;l Academy of Sciences doi:10.1073/pnas.1012095107</p>               <p>2 Stokes-Rees, Sliz, Compute and data management                 strategies for grid deployment of high throughput                 protein structure studies, IEEE Workshop on Many Task                 Computing on Grids and Supercomputers 2010 (MTAGS10),                 Seattle, November 2010.</p>               <p>~ Ian Stokes-Rees </p>               <hr />                              <b>HCC Use Case on OSG </b>               <p>The Holland Computing Center (HCC) successfully                 completed a &ldquo;burst&rdquo; run of 168K wall hours over the                 weekend of November 6 for a local medical researcher                 using OSG. At the beginning of November, the researcher                 came to us with a deadline to finish his work by                 November 15. He completed ahead of schedule. </p>               <p>Several things came together to make this happen: </p>               <div>                 <p>&ndash; HCC staff assisted the researchers in converting                   his workflow to run on Condor. </p>                 <p>&ndash; glideinWMS and Condor powered the underlying submit                   infrastructure and to stably delivered 6,000 running                   jobs from a single submit node. </p>                 <p>&ndash; SRM and GridFTP were used to deliver input files                   (about 150MB / job). </p>                 <p>&ndash; HCC was able to opportunistically use many OSG                   sites. Previously, HCC clusters provided about half                   the resources the HCC VO utilized; this time, they                   were closer to a quarter.</p>                 <p> Several required items did not work well, but were                   eventually overcome: &ndash; Our glideinWMS node                   destabilized above 6,000 jobs or when Condor was used                   in flocking mode. </p>                 <p>&ndash; One GridFTP server crashed during the run, taking                   many jobs with it. SRM filled its disk partition with                   log files and crashed. When a GridFTP or SRM endpoint                   dies, things start to fail. We will be looking into                   fixing or mitigating these issues for the &ldquo;next time,&rdquo;                   whenever that might be. </p>                 <p>Please view the <a href="http://t2.unl.edu/gratia/bar_graphs/facility_hours_bar_smry?vo=hcc&amp;group=resource_group&amp;endtime=2010-11-09+23%3A59%3A59&amp;starttime=2010-11-05+00%3A00%3A00%20">results</a>.</p>                 <p>~ Brian Bockelman </p>               </div>               <hr />               <p><b>OSG Participation in the CLCAR 2010</b></p>               <p>The Conferencia Latinoamericana en Computacion de Alto                 Rendimiento (CLCAR) is an annual Latin American workshop                 on high performance computing. Although it focuses on                 HPC, grid and cloud computing are also becoming topics                 of interest. The latest workshop, held in August in                 Gramado, Brazil, included a panel discussion about which                 grid middleware is feasible for the regional national                 grid infrastructures in development. South American                 researchers are considering an international grid                 infrastructure for all of South America as a unique                 platform or as a federation of medium-size grid                 projects. Two major platforms were presented during the                 discussion panel: gLite by Diego Carvalho and OSG by                 myself. My presentation familiarized the Latin American                 community with OSG, and several countries have joined                 the OSG outreach effort in the region.</p>               <p>~ Jose Caballero</p>               <hr />               <p><b>Executive Director&#39;s Report</b></p>               <div>                 <p>Production continues to be smooth for both physics                   and non-physics applications. We are gearing up for                   another round of collecting information about research                   and results that have benefited from OSG services,                   such as software, expertise, resources, etc.</p>                 <p>The OSG visualization at SC10 went well. If you are                   interested in using this visualization in other venues                   you can see a <a href="https://twiki.grid.iu.edu/bin/viewauth/Main/SuperComputingProposal">description</a>.                   We were able to connect with new users and demonstrate                   earthquake engineering simulation and gravitational                   wave simulation applications for the first time across                   several OSG sites.</p>                 <p>~ Ruth Pordes</p>               </div>               <p>&nbsp;</p>             </td>             <td valign="top">               <p><b>South American Grid School</b></p>               <p><a href="/images/DSC00039.jpg" target="_blank"><img src="/images/DSC00039.jpg" alt="Grid School" longdesc="http://www-hep.nhn.ou.edu/pics/osg-0910/IMG_1824.JPG" align="left" height="105" width="140" /></a>The most                 recent OSG grid school was held the first week of                 December in Sao Paulo, Brazil. Around 40 students                 attended from multiple science disciplines and 23                 institutions. The school, focused on end-users&rsquo; needs,                 taught students step by step how to join the Grid                 community, adapt their scientific applications to the                 new technology and use it efficiently. Also, students                 learned how to run large-scale computing applications,                 first locally and then using the OSG.<br />                 Two open discussions took place, along with one Q&amp;A                 session with Ruth Pordes and Miron Livny and hands-on                 exercises. Other OSG staff who lectured included Dan                 Fraser, Tanya Levshina, Zack Miller, Igor Sfiligoi. Joel                 Snow lectured on the experience of the D0 high-energy                 physics experiment using OSG resources, enabling                 attendees to see how all the concepts and techniques                 they learned during the week have been applied by a real                 experiment. A working meeting between the GOC&rsquo;s Rob                 Quick and those developing and deploying a local grid                 operation center allowed the sharing of ideas and                 experiences. Two special lectures by invited local                 speakers, one on high performance computing and one on                 data storage, completed the school. </p>               <p>Some comments from students:</p>               <p>&ldquo;The OSG School in Sao Paulo was very good. The Grid                 concepts were presented in a theoretical and practical                 way, with a focus on the main goal of any Grid                 infrastructure: &lsquo;collaboration.&rsquo;<br />                 - Charles Rodamilans, PhD student of Computer                 Engineering</p>               <p>&ldquo;&hellip; the most impressive course that I have attended, not                 because of the size of the Open Science Grid, but                 because of the ability and motivation that instructors                 demonstrated throughout the course. They were                 informative and technically knowledgeable. &ldquo;<br />                 - Eriksen Costa, Software architect</p>               <p>&ldquo;The school was fantastic! &hellip; It gave me a broadened                 view of what a grid is, what it is useful for and how I                 can use it.&rdquo;<a href="/images/DSC00049.jpg"><img src="/images/DSC00049.jpg" alt="Grid School" longdesc="https://1725344785908837487-a-1802744773732722657-s-sites.googlegroups.com/site/jcaballeroosg/docs/DSC00039.JPG?attachauth=ANoY7cqU9VvAj19RPZGc7ZY2Yd8xzUQClOK-hTLgcf39VH-IOAaqR2om9ehKQGvhEM41guaS16ZSsk6mK5xbEDyjGEu3PxG9CZx_lDs7hwPFreghs9khsf0Utp-32tLctGVg6f6YkaZJfyZeXTW7x-uJ5QVmo12IRAolMq6DOreJzG6BSkC-uZKNrqd4lheIpo5w9Qe4Fat1qvbGJCUd9NDHytaXscpZnA%3D%3D&amp;attredirects=0" align="right" height="105" width="140" /></a><br />                 - Griselda Garrido, neuroscience researcher</p>               <p>~ Jose Caballero</p>               <p>&nbsp;</p>               <hr />               <p><b>Production Report for CMS</b></p>               <p>CMS has wrapped up the 2010 proton-proton run, taking                 advantage of the earnest efforts of the LHC to                 continually increase luminosity. We recorded more than                 43 pb^-1 during the run and are working hard on                 analysis. The fun isn&rsquo;t over yet, though &mdash; the machine                 has now started on the heavy ion program, and has ramped                 from zero collisions to 120&times;120 bunches in a week! And                 on November 18th, we announced the detection of Z bosons                 from the heavy ion program &mdash; This the first time Z                 bosons have been observed in heavy ion collisions. We                 look forward to the coming weeks as we accumulate more                 data!</p>               <p>~ Burt Holzman </p>               <hr />               <p><b>Improving Documentation</b></p>               <p>Composing great technical documentation--documentation                 that reassures the reader that they will achieve their                 goals efficiently by following the instructions                 presented--requires patience to review and test text                 that will then be gradually improved in an iterative                 process.</p>               <p><a href="https://twiki.grid.iu.edu/bin/view/Trash/DocumentationTeam/WebHome">The                   Content Management Project</a> was founded in                 September 2009 with the goal to improve the                 documentation released with <a href="https://twiki.grid.iu.edu/bin/view/ReleaseDocumentation%20">OSG                   software and tools</a> . Since then, 419 documents                 have cleaned up and reduced to 277 documents that have                 been integrated into a <a href="https://twiki.grid.iu.edu/bin/view/ReleaseDocumentation/DocProcess">peer-review-like                   process</a>. Since April, a documentation team from                 the OSG community has led the review, improvement and                 release of 164 of these documents, 59% of all documents                 involved in the process.</p>               <p>The Content Management Project is a community project                 of the Open Science Grid. As such its success entirely                 depends on volunteers picking up the many small                 documentation tasks. If you&rsquo;ve ever wished for better                 OSG documentation, please help us to make it happen! We                 are constantly looking for people who would like take <a href="https://twiki.grid.iu.edu/bin/view/ReleaseDocumentation/DocOwnerResponsibilities">ownership                   of a document</a> and who will, in turn, be supported                 and guided by members of the documentation team while                 improving the document <a href="mailto:osg-docs@listserv.fnal.gov">osg-docs@listserv.fnal.gov</a>.</p>               <p>~ Robert Engel</p>               <hr />               <p><b>VO Forum Update </b></p>               <p>We are striving to bring in more technical experts to                 present tools and information that may be of use to VOs.                 On Dec. 2, Tech-X Corporation representatives presented                 an introduction on SVOPME, a collaborative project                 between Tech-X and Fermilab to provide a scalable                 privileges management environment. SVOPME consists of                 tools for VOs to define and publish desired resource                 access privileges and for sites to assist in configuring                 appropriate access policies. The project is looking for                 VOs who are interested in using this <a href="https://twiki.grid.iu.edu/twiki/pub/VirtualOrganizations/VOGroupMeeting20101202/SVOPME-Introduction-VO.pdf.">technology</a>.               </p>               <p>In addition, OSG Storage team member Doug Strain,                 developer of <a href="http://home.fnal.gov/%7Edstrain/pigeon/">Pigeon                   Tools</a> recently presented at this forum. Pigeon                 Tools is a method of easily creating extendable classes                 of tests to evaluate the viability of SEs for VOs. <br />                 <br />                 The CDF Offline team is working out issues on running                 their data processing jobs at the Korea Institute of                 Science and Technology Information (KISTI), which                 provides dedicated resources to CDF.</p>               <p>The Large Synoptic Survey Telescope (LSST) recently                 finished Phase 2 of their run on OSG, simulating an                 entire night of telescope observation (1000 images with                 the 3200 MegaPixel camera). The LSST simulation group                 successfully performed image validation and are now                 planning the next phase.</p>               <p>Gearing up for their December 17 software release,                 GEANT-4 is conducting validation testing using                 opportunistic computing on OSG at half a dozen sites. </p>               <p>The Holland Computing Center (HCC) successfully                 completed a priority workflow in early November. 110,000                 CPU hours, 26 TB were transferred.
