<table cellPadding="0" cellSpacing="10" border="0" bgColor="#ffffcc" width="100%"><tbody><tr bgColor="#ffffcc" borderColor="#999999"><td vAlign="middle" width="146"><p><a target="_blank" href="http://www.opensciencegrid.org/images/osg-logo.gif"><img longDesc="https//www.opensciencegrid.org/images/osg_logo.gif" border="0" src="http://www.opensciencegrid.org/images/osg-logo.gif" alt="OSG Logo" align="middle" height="82" width="144" /></a></p><br /></td><td vAlign="top" align="left"><!-- TemplateBeginEditable name="issueDate" --><h1>OSG Newsletter, September 2011</h1><!-- TemplateEndEditable --></td></tr></tbody></table><table cellPadding="0" cellSpacing="10" border="0" bgColor="#fdebcf" width="100%"><tbody><tr><!-- TemplateBeginEditable name="body" --><td vAlign="top" align="left" width="50%"><h2>The Great Software Migration: From Pacman to RPMs</h2><p>Hopefully most of you are now familiar with the current project to migrate the OSG Software Stack (VDT) from using Pacman to using RPMs (RPM Package Manager) for installation. This is a substantial migration that has a variety of impacts on anyone that installs the software. </p><p>There are two things you need to know. First, we have stopped scheduled updates of the Pacman-based VDT. We will only ship urgent updates, such as security fixes. The Pacman caches will remain for as long as needed so you may continue to use them. However, they will not be updated to support new software or new platforms. </p><p>Second, by December we will release most of the VDT in RPM form for Scientific Linux 5, Centos 5, and Red Hat Enterprise Linux 5. In particular, we will release: </p><blockquote><p>&bull;The client and worker node client (including glexec) </p><p>&bull;The CE and RSV </p><p>&bull;VOMS and VOMS Admin </p><p>&bull;Bestman, Xrootd, and HDFS </p></blockquote><p>Much of the preliminary work is done, and we are beginning to do wider testing. We have started testing in the Integration Testbed as well as with a few sites who have volunteered to act as beta testers.</p><p>In the next few months you will hear a lot more about the testing. We will be looking for more VOs to help us with testing, particularly to validate that their scientific workflows haven&#39;t unexpectedly broken due to the new software distribution. </p><p>Part of our goal in moving to RPMs is to leverage the work that others have done in packaging the software that we use. This means that in some cases we are moving to new versions of the software. For example, we will be moving to Globus 5.2 and VOMS 2.0. </p><p>If you&#39;d like to read more about this transition, we have documentation, including: </p><blockquote><p>&bull;Documents about the <font color="#0066cc"><a target="_blank" href="https://twiki.grid.iu.edu/bin/view/SoftwareTeam/RPMTransition">RPM Transition</a></font>, including our regularly-updated timeline. </p><p>&bull;The <a target="_blank" href="https://indico.fnal.gov/materialDisplay.py?contribId=4&amp;sessionId=0&amp;materialId=slides&amp;confId=4531"><font color="#0066cc">plenary talk</font></a> at the OSG Summer Workshop on the transition. </p><p>&bull;A <a href="https://indico.fnal.gov/contributionDisplay.py?contribId=17&amp;sessionId=3&amp;confId=4531"><font color="#0066cc">technical talk</font></a> from the OSG Summer Workshop on installing the VDT via RPMs.</p></blockquote><p>If you have any questions or concerns, please do not hesitate to contact <a href="mailto:roy@cs.wisc.edu"><font color="#0066cc">Alain Roy </font></a>, the OSG Software Coordinator</p><p>~Alain Roy</p><hr /><h2>GlueX Research</h2><p>The Gluonic Excitations Experiment (<a href="http://www.gluex.org/"><font color="#800080">GlueX</font></a>) is a major new initiative to explore the physics of strong interactions at the interface between nuclear physics, the world of neutrons and protons, and the Standard Model of high-energy physics, where the nuclear elementary particles are quarks and gluons. </p><p>GlueX is currently being constructed at the Thomas Jefferson National Accelerator Facility in Newport News, Virginia, as a part of the upgrade of the electron accelerator to 12 GeV. When physics data taking begins in 2015, the experiment will register particle interactions at a rate of 20 kHz, and demand the capability to record 300 MB/s, with a total yield of two petabytes per year. </p><p>In parallel with real data collection and processing, a vast amount of simulated data is also required in order to understand the response of the detector to the particles that pass through it. Approximately half of the 80 million CPU hours required per year to process and analyze the data from the GlueX spectrometer will be devoted to Monte Carlo simulations. </p><p>This is a task which is well adapted to the capabilities of the Open Science Grid. As a step toward this ultimate goal of 40 million CPU hours per year, GlueX has launched an initial simulation exercise on the OSG aimed at generating and analyzing one full month&rsquo;s worth of simulation data from GlueX by the end of 2011, requiring approximately one million CPU hours. This data will be hosted on OSG storage deployed by the GlueX VO; it will be used to refine the analysis cuts and algorithms we will use when we begin to take physics data. This head-start will maximize our chances for early discovery once the experiment comes online. </p><p>~Richard Jones<br /></p><hr /><h2><strong>ATLAS Update </strong></h2><p>Production, analysis, and Tier-0 processing has been running smoothly across the grid. Over the course of the last month about 15 million Monte Carlo, group, reprocessing, and analysis jobs were processed through the PanDA workload management system and completed successfully on the WLCG computing resources available to ATLAS worldwide. About 4.5 million jobs were processed on OSG resources. </p><p>Using a new software release that incorporates major improvements, ATLAS is currently in the middle of a reprocessing campaign. The campaign will reprocess all raw data taken in 2011 so far -- approximately seven billion events. The U.S. ATLAS Tier-1 center at Brookhaven National Laboratory is processing 27% of the total raw data from this campaign. </p><p>~Michael Ernst<br /></p><hr /><h2>From the Executive Director</h2><p>This month sees the departure of Robert Engel who has been active over the past few years running LIGO Einstein@HOME across many of the OSG sites, maintaining the integration Test bed site at Caltech, and as the coordinator of producing consistent and up to date documentation on the Twiki. We wish Robert all the best for his future path. </p><p>~Ruth Pordes</p><hr /><h2>ATLAS and CMS Thumbnails</h2><table border="1"><tbody><tr></tr></tbody></table><table border="0" width="100"><tbody><tr><td><a href="/images/ATLAS.png"><img longDesc="https://www.opensciencegrid.org/images/ATLAS_aug11.png" src="/images/ATLAS_aug11.png" alt="ATLAS Thumbnails" height="139" width="180" /></a></td><td><a href="/images/CMS.png"><img longDesc="https://www.opensciencegrid.org/images/ATLAS_aug11.png" src="/images/CMS_aug11.png" alt="CMS Thumbnails" height="139" width="180" /></a></td></tr></tbody></table><hr /><h2>OSG Document Database updated in the Last 30 Days </h2><table cellPadding="0" cellSpacing="0" border="0"><tbody><tr><td vAlign="bottom"><p align="center">OSG-doc-#</p></td><td vAlign="bottom"><p align="center">Title</p></td><td vAlign="bottom"><p align="center">Author(s)</p></td><td vAlign="bottom"><p align="center">Topic(s)</p></td><td vAlign="bottom"><p align="center">Last Updated</p></td></tr><tr><td vAlign="top"><p><a href="http://osg-docdb.opensciencegrid.org/cgi-bin/ShowDocument?docid=1074" title="OSG-doc-1074-v1"><font color="#0066cc">1074-v1</font></a></p></td><td vAlign="top"><p><a href="http://osg-docdb.opensciencegrid.org/cgi-bin/ShowDocument?docid=1074" title="OSG-doc-1074-v1"><font color="#0066cc">US LHC Tier2 Activity for August 2011</font></a></p></td><td vAlign="top"><p><a href="http://osg-docdb.opensciencegrid.org/cgi-bin/ListBy?authorid=381" title="University of Nebraska"><font color="#0066cc">Adam Caprez</font></a></p></td><td vAlign="top"><p><a href="http://osg-docdb.opensciencegrid.org/cgi-bin/ListBy?topicid=76" title="Activities:Metrics"><font color="#0066cc">Metrics</font></a></p></td><td vAlign="top" noWrap="nowrap"><p align="right">21 Sep 2011</p></td></tr></tbody></table><p>&nbsp;</p></td><td vAlign="top" width="50%"><h2>Featured Site: ATLAS Great Lakes Tier-2</h2><div style="width: 180px; padding-right: 10px; float: left" class="caption"><img src="/images/AGLT2UM.JPG" alt="AGLT2UM" height="121" width="180" />&nbsp; <div style="width: 180px"><em>University of Michigan, Rob Isenberg, Robert (Bob) Ball (AGT2 Manager), Shawn McKee (AGLT2) Director, Ben Meekhof. We are standing in front of an ATLAS Live display (broadcast from CERN) showing an event simulation in the ATLAS detector. Image courtesy of University of Michigan</em>, <em>Please view more of the ATLAS <font color="#800080">Team</font></em></div></div><p><strong>Site name:</strong> AGLT2 (ATLAS Great Lakes Tier-2) <br /><strong>Location:</strong> Ann Arbor and East Lansing, Michigan <br /><strong>Owner: </strong>USATLAS <br /><strong>Cores:</strong> 4500<br /><strong>Storage:</strong> 2.2 Petabytes of disk in dCache <br /><strong>Virtual Organizations:</strong> Home to USATLAS <br /><strong>Used by:</strong> LIGO; HCC; SBGRID; SURAGRID; GPN; GLOW; GEANT4; OSG; ENGAGE VOs <br /><strong>Opportunistic access:</strong> Up to 25 cores to members of other VOs </p><p><strong>What is unusual about your site and the challenges you face?</strong> <br />We are split over two physical locations that are 70 miles apart, yet the site is treated as a single resource and appears as such to our users. We rely on the availability of the long distance network links to keep us up. Although the links are redundant, failures can occur that take the whole site offline. </p><p><strong>What projects are you working on now?<br /></strong>We&rsquo;re working on several projects right now. For example, improving network performance is particularly crucial for our site, and so we&rsquo;re exploring the integrated use of networking via projects like DYNES and UltraLight. </p><p><strong>What is the best part of collaborating with and contributing to OSG? </strong><br />They are attentive to problems that inevitably occur and good about fixing them. They pay attention to the needs of the community in providing their software through various mechanisms. </p><hr /><h2>Using xrootd for &quot;Any Data, Anytime, Anywhere&quot;</h2><p>The CMS experiment is embarking on an ambitious extension to its computing model by adding &quot;Any Data, Anytime, Anywhere&quot; capabilities. </p><p>At present, to run a job, users generally submit a batch job via the CMS workload management system to a grid site that has the data registered. This makes interactive debugging difficult, as the data of interest first has to be extracted out of the system before it can be visualized or inspected. </p><p>To provide remote access to any data, at any time, from anywhere, CMS is integrating a software layer that allows global redirection from a single address and remote access on top of its global data grid. The software implementation chosen is xrootd, software originally developed for the BaBar experiment at SLAC, and now jointly maintained and developed by a collaboration between SLAC, CERN, and universities on both sides of the Atlantic. An initial prototype is now deployed, and initial user responses are very positive.</p><p>In addition to interactive access, CMS is envisioning simplifying Tier-3 operations with this new technology by allowing Tier-3 CPUs to access any data from a regional xrootd redirection system, and to develop an overflow logic. For the latter, we intend to send jobs to sites that don&#39;t have the data if the site that has it is backlogged with jobs pending for more than six hours or so. The number of jobs that &quot;overflow&quot; is regulated via glideinWMS such as to not overextend the WAN access capabilities of the source site. </p><p>In the US, we expect to build such a system comprising the Tier-2s and the LHC Physics Center at FNAL. Similar regional systems are envisioned in other parts of the world. The &quot;Any Data, Anytime, Anywhere&quot; initiative is a joint effort between ATLAS, CMS, OSG, and CERN, with the goal to bring this capability to other scientific communities, after it has proven itself at the LHC. This would allow scientific communities to &quot;import&quot; data somewhere into OSG, and then use that data from anywhere on the OSG distributed infrastructure. </p><p>~Frank Wuerthwein</p><hr /><h2>OSG: There&#39;s an (Android) app for that</h2><p><img longDesc="https://www.opensciencegrid.org/images/OSGdroid2pic.png" src="/images/OSGdroid2pic.png" alt="OSG Android App" align="left" height="180" width="108" />A few weekends ago, I decided it was a good time to learn something new. So, I spent some time putting together an OSG Android app. Not all the features are working yet, but I tend to release early and often on all my projects. </p><p>Part of my motivation was the upcoming SC11 conference in mid-November. My <a href="https://twiki.grid.iu.edu/bin/view/Main/InstallingOSGGoogleEarth"><font color="#800080">visualization</font></a> from last year has only improved incrementally, so I wanted to bring something new to Seattle, where this year&#39;s conference takes place. Everyone knows the shinier the toys at SC, the better. Who knows; maybe I can use a tablet and show some cool things to people walking by. </p><p>So far, the working features are: </p><blockquote><p>&bull;Automatically updated global usage data on the home screen <br />&bull;Usage queried from gratia for every site and VO <br />&bull;Graphing of the usage data (though I want to work on that some more) </p></blockquote><p>Some features I want to work on more: </p><blockquote><p>&bull;Get the map to show sites across the US. Whether it shows RSV data (boring) or usage data (slightly better), I&#39;m not sure yet. <br />&bull;Get a better looking home screen. It seems too &#39;black&#39;. </p></blockquote><p>You can get it now on the Android market. Search for &#39;osg.&#39; It works well on my 2.3.3 phone, the 2.2 emulator, and the Android 3.X tablet emulator. This increases the probability of so-called &quot;pile-up events&quot; &ndash; bunch crossings that yield more than one proton-proton collision.
